**Re: Submission of manuscript "Empiria: A Modular-Synthesizer Platform for Simulation-Based Statistics Education"**

Dear Editor,

I am pleased to submit the enclosed manuscript for consideration as
an Article in the *Journal of Statistics and Data Science Education*.

The paper describes the **Methods** plugin of **Empiria**, an
open-source (GPL-3.0) suite for VCV Rack 2 — a free
modular-synthesizer environment that I have repurposed as a live,
signal-flow teaching surface for statistics. Methods comprises
fourteen modules that together expose a full inferential pipeline
— data-generating process → sample-window measurement → estimation
→ inference → diagnostics → reporting — as patch cables on a rack.
The paper sets the design in the context of two decades of
GAISE-aligned calls for simulation-based reasoning [Cobb 2007;
GAISE 2016; Tintle et al. 2014], the constructionist heritage of
Papert and Resnick, and the older tradition of physical statistical
instruments (the Galton board, the analog computer, the
chemical-plant trainer).

The contribution sits squarely within JSDSE's scope. The plugin
spans the four competencies the journal regularly addresses —
sampling-distribution intuition, simulation-based inference,
regression, and reproducible computational workflows — and is
designed for use in classrooms ranging from high-school general
education to graduate research-methods seminars. The implementation
draws on the established numerical literature (Lentz continued
fractions for the incomplete beta in the *t*-test [Press et al.
2007]; Efron's bias-corrected and accelerated bootstrap [Efron
1987]; Sanger's rule for online PCA [Sanger 1989]) so that the
underlying numerics are auditable rather than implicit. To keep
the contribution focused, the paper describes five **anchor
modules** (*Sample*, *Frame*, *Regress*, *Test*, *Boot*) that
carry the simulation-based-inference argument in depth, and
summarises the remaining nine Methods modules in a compact
reference table that points to the project manual for full detail.

Empiria's broader suite — four additional plugins covering
agent-based social models, network epidemiology, spatial dynamics,
and behavioral-economics models — shares the Methods plugin's
panel grammar, patch-cable conventions, and deterministic-seed
reproducibility story. To keep the JSDSE submission narrowly
focused on statistics education, those plugins are noted only
briefly here (with one cross-plugin Schelling demonstration in
Workflow A to illustrate that the same signal-flow approach
generalises) and are the subject of a separate companion paper
for the computational social-science community.

Three worked examples in the paper illustrate the range of use
cases: (a) a visceral Schelling-segregation demonstration suitable
for introductory teaching; (b) a fully seeded, reproducible
two-sample *t*-test pipeline; and (c) an empirical verification of
a closed-form probability (four-coin-flip Bernoulli runs) that
makes the case for methodological robustness explicit. A dedicated
"Methodological robustness" section documents the numerical
algorithms used and their references, addressing the legitimate
concern that an unconventional environment might trade rigor for
appeal.

The manuscript is approximately 8,500 words. All software, panels,
documentation, and three starter patches generated by an included
Python script are available under GPL-3.0 at the URL listed on the
title page. The paper has not been previously published and is not
under consideration elsewhere. I am the sole author and have no
conflicts of interest to declare.

I have suggested five reviewers (listed separately) with expertise
spanning statistics education, computational social science,
simulation-based inference, and open-source educational software. I
would be grateful for the opportunity to have this work assessed
by the JSDSE community, whose readership most closely matches the
audience I have built Empiria for.

Thank you for your consideration.
